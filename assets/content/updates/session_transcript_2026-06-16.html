Good time to decide this — both the threshold sweep and the normalization-stats experiment are forms of fitting to data, so they need to be governed by the same split discipline as fine-tuning.

My recommendation: 3-way split, 9 train / 2 val / 3 test

  • Test (3 scenes): Lock these away now, never look at them until you want a final number to report. Pick scenes that are diverse (different times of year, viewing geometry, contrail density) so the test score is representative.
  • Val (2 scenes): Use these to pick the threshold, compute normalization stats, and (if you fine-tune) do early stopping / model selection. They're "tuning budget" — you can look at them, but they don't go in the loss.
  • Train (7 + the small scene = 8): Everything else. Fine-tuning gradients come only from here.

Why not train/test only? The threshold sweep and normalization stats are low-DF fits, so they won't badly overfit 9 scenes — but if you iterate even a little (try a few threshold values, compare norm strategies), the train set score stops being a fair estimate and you'll want the validation signal anyway. Better to designate it upfront.

Why not a bigger test set? 3 scenes is already thin, but with large scenes you have millions of pixels per scene, so 3 gives you a statistically stable IoU estimate. Shrinking it to 2 saves little; expanding to 4 eats into your already small training pool.

The small scene: Put it in train. It contributes less signal for evaluation but is fine as a training example, and you don't want your test set to be dominated by an unrepresentative tiny scene.

One practical note: before you assign scenes, look at the metadata — acquisition time, orbit path, geographic region. If two scenes are from the same orbit pass or same day over the same area, they're not independent and should be in the same split. You want the 3 test scenes to be as diverse from each other and from train as possible.

What do you know about the 12 scenes — times, regions, anything like that? That would let us make concrete split assignments.

#142026-06-15T19:19:40.679Z

Are you currently applying 0.5 as a threshold instead of the model value?

2 bash

Yes and no — there are two thresholds in play:

  1. detect_contrails applies md.threshold (0.42) internally — it thresholds the softmax output before returning, so raw_mask is already a boolean array.

  2. The > 0.5 on line 111 is applied to the reprojected values. Since raw_mask is cast to float32 (0.0/1.0) and reprojected with nearest-neighbor, the values after reprojection are also 0.0 or 1.0 (plus NaN for gaps). So > 0.5 is effectively == 1.0 and has no practical effect — it's just converting back to bool.

So the model's 0.42 threshold is what's actually controlling detections, and the 0.5 is harmless but misleading.

However, this is a problem for the planned threshold sweep: detect_contrails bakes the threshold in and discards the raw probabilities. To sweep the threshold, you'll need to extract the softmax output before the >= md.threshold step. That means either duplicating the model inference code or adding a return_probs=True path. Worth sorting out before starting the sweep.

#212026-06-15T20:09:43.781Z

Okay, let's brainstorm the preprocessing. I want to download quite a bit of VIIRS data to calculate the means and standard deviations in the relevant channels. How do I go about randomly picking swaths in different locations at different times of day and with different conditions? I already have a function (in a different repo) to downlaod VIIRS data

The main axes of variation you care about for BT statistics are:

Season — BT distributions shift significantly with season (surface and cloud-top temperatures). Stratify by month or at minimum by quarter.

Latitude — The dominant driver of BT magnitude. Tropical scenes are systematically warmer than polar ones. Sample across latitude bands (e.g. 60°S–30°S, 30°S–30°N, 30°N–60°N, 60°N–90°N).

Land vs. ocean — Different surface emissivities and thermal inertia shift the distribution, especially at night.

Cloud cover — Don't try to control for this; just let it be random. The model will see all cloud fractions, so you want your stats to reflect the full mix.

Day/night overpass — Since you're on thermal IR channels the effect is smaller than for solar channels, but nighttime land temperatures can differ enough to matter.

Practical approach: build a stratified sample grid of (month × latitude band × land/ocean) cells, then for each cell pick a small number of granules randomly from whatever is available. You don't need many — BT statistics converge fast given each swath is ~20M pixels. 5–10 granules per cell is plenty, so even a coarse 4-season × 4-latitude-band × 2-surface grid gives ~80–160 swaths total, which is more than enough.

The key question for implementation: what does your download function take as input — a granule ID, a date+bounding box, or something else? That determines whether you query LAADS first to get a candidate list to sample from, or whether you generate random timestamps and locations and resolve them to granule IDs.

CONUS overlap fraction — filter first, before any sampling. Set a minimum threshold (e.g. ≥ 0.3) to exclude granules that barely clip the edge. For normalization stats you want granules that are meaningfully over CONUS, not just grazing it.

File names — pass directly to your download function once you've selected the sample.

Both are straightforward from what you have.

Location — take the centroid longitude of the four corners and bin it:

gdf['centroid_lon'] = gdf.geometry.centroid.x
gdf['location'] = pd.cut(
    gdf['centroid_lon'],
    bins=[-180, -105, -85, 0],
    labels=['west', 'central', 'east']
)

Rough boundaries: ~105°W splits the Rockies from the Plains, ~85°W splits the Plains from the East Coast.

Time of day — compute local solar time from the UTC granule time and centroid longitude, no extra packages needed:

gdf['centroid_lon'] = gdf.geometry.centroid.x  # if not already done
gdf['local_solar_hour'] = (
    gdf['granule_time'].dt.hour
    + gdf['granule_time'].dt.minute / 60
    + gdf['centroid_lon'] / 15
) % 24
gdf['time_of_day'] = (gdf['local_solar_hour'].between(6, 18)).map({True: 'day', False: 'night'})

This is accurate enough — sun-synchronous orbits are regular enough that you won't get ambiguous cases near the 6/18 boundary in practice.

After these two columns are set you have a clean (season, location, time_of_day) stratification to sample from. Do you want three or two location bins?
