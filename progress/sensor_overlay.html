Spotlight · Summer 2026

Sensor overlay

Combining observations from multiple platforms — GOES ABI, VIIRS, ground cameras, lidar, and radiosondes — into a combined and interactive sensor overlay tool.

No single sensor captures the full picture of a contrail's formation and persistence. Geostationary satellites offer temporal continuity; low-Earth orbiters provide sharper spatial detail across large spatial coverage; ground cameras give a direct visual reference at the time of formation; lidars resolve the vertical structure and ice crystal properties; and radiosondes characterize the atmospheric conditions that govern persistence. Overlaying these sources onto a shared coordinate frame makes it possible to identify the same contrail across instruments and link its observable properties to the underlying meteorology. As a starting point, this tool shows the availability of data from various sensors over the MIT Sky Camera for the past seven days.

Overview

Currently included sensors

The tool currently integrates four observation sources. Each covers a different spatial and temporal scale, and together they form the foundation of the multi-sensor observational framework.

Sensor Platform Cadence Coverage
MIT Sky Camera Ground (MIT campus) Continuous Fisheye hemispheric sky view; always on
GOES ABI Geostationary (GOES-16/18) 5-minute CONUS Full eastern US; mcast contrail detections filtered to camera FOV
VIIRS Low-Earth orbit (Suomi-NPP, NOAA-20/21) ~14 orbits / day 750 m resolution swath; overpasses over camera FOV logged
WindBorne Drifting stratospheric balloons (WindBorne Systems, live API) Opportunistic — as balloons drift through ROI transits logged; relative humidity over ice (RHi) & ISSR flagged per level

Note: consecutive orbits of a given VIIRS satellite are roughly 100 minutes apart and overlap spatially, which is why each platform can contribute more than one swath over a region of interest per day. Overpasses cycle through the constellation in a fixed order — Suomi NPP, NOAA-20, NOAA-21, Suomi NPP, and so on — with each successive swath shifted slightly west of the one before it.

Note: for WindBorne, "ROI" (region of interest) refers to a 1000 km × 1000 km bounding box centered on the MIT Sky Camera (500 km north, east, south, and west) — not the same 50 km radius used to filter the other sensors above. A WindBorne sounding is logged whenever its balloon's drift track passes through this box, regardless of where the balloon launched. An ISSR detection flag does not automatically apply to all measurements with RHi > 1, but also requires clear-sky (RH < 1), and is restricted to the cruise altitude range (FL250-FL450). Both ISSRs and interlayers with less than 100 ft vertical extent are ignored and merged into the adjacent clear air mass or ISSR, respectively.

What's Next

Upcoming sensors

The following observation sources are in preparation and will be added to the overlay tool as data pipelines are finalized.

  • Radiosondes — GRUAN. Atmospheric profiles from the GCOS Reference Upper-Air Network (GRUAN) will provide additional vertical temperature and humidity structure alongside the WindBorne soundings already shown above, to further characterize the atmospheric state at contrail formation times.
  • EarthCARE. Swath overpasses from ESA's EarthCARE satellite (launched 2024) will contribute collocated lidar profiles of ice cloud vertical structure, including potential contrail detections in the vertical dimension.
  • VIIRS detections. Contrail detections derived from VIIRS imagery will be added as individual markers on the VIIRS overpass row, analogous to the ABI detection marks already shown for GOES. Where a VIIRS overpass coincides with a known contrail, the detection will link to the corresponding imagery.
  • AMDAR / IAGOS flight tracks. Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR) and IAGOS in-situ measurements from commercial aircraft will supply flight-level atmospheric observations, helping link the meteorological environment to contrail observable properties.
